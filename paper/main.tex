\title{When to Forecast End-to-End versus Impute-then-Forecast:
A Measured-Imputability Crossover for Air-Quality Prediction on
Incomplete Monitoring Networks}

\author[a]{Anonymous Author}
\address[a]{Anonymous Institution}

\begin{abstract}
Operational air-quality monitoring networks produce severely incomplete
multivariate streams, yet the prevailing practice is a two-stage
\emph{impute-then-forecast} pipeline whose imputation stage must be refit and
re-run on every data refresh. We ask a question that the literature has left
implicit: \emph{when} should one forecast end-to-end from masked inputs rather
than impute first? We answer it with a controlled study on three monitoring
networks that span the reconstructability spectrum---Dhaka, Bangladesh (\pmf{}
\SI{23.7}{\percent} missing), the Delhi CPCB network (published complete but
highly noisy), and the Beijing Multi-Site benchmark (\SI{2.1}{\percent}
missing)---using a deliberately simple, CPU-deployable missingness-aware
Transformer that consumes the observation mask natively. Against strong baselines
including the missingness-native GRU-D recurrent model and three
impute-then-forecast pipelines (KNN, MICE, and the deep self-attention imputer
SAITS), the end-to-end model reaches \emph{accuracy parity} at every horizon on
the incomplete networks; per-seed Diebold--Mariano tests confirm no significant
difference, correcting a single-seed result in earlier work that did not
replicate. Our central contribution is to make the choice \emph{predictive}: we
define a directly \textbf{measured imputability} of a network---one minus the
ratio of a deep imputer's to forward-fill's reconstruction error on held-out
observed cells---and use it as a decision statistic. Across the
three networks the advantage falls from \SI{+1.7}{\ugm} on least-imputable Dhaka
(imputability $-0.39$) through \SI{-2.6}{\ugm} on Delhi ($-0.09$) to
\SI{-4.3}{\ugm} on most-imputable Beijing ($+0.20$), yielding a deployable rule:
\emph{measure a network's imputability, then choose the paradigm}---forecast
end-to-end (with training-time missingness dropout, which also wins on
high-pollution episodes) in the low-imputability regime, and impute-then-forecast
otherwise. The mechanism is consistent: on Delhi and Beijing the deep imputer is
reconstructable enough to win (on Delhi it does not even pass its forward-fill
quality gate), whereas on noisy, severely incomplete Dhaka it has little to
exploit and end-to-end takes over. We provide the imputability crossover as a
deployment decision rule, a fully reproducible multi-seed benchmark across three
networks, and a data-quality lesson on sensor error codes that, left unhandled,
distort every reported number.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Fine particulate matter (\pmf) is among the largest environmental risk factors
for human health \citep{who2021guidelines,kim2009review}, and short-horizon
\pmf{} forecasts drive public advisories and exposure mitigation. The data that
must feed those forecasts, however, are produced by physical monitoring networks
that are routinely and severely incomplete: sensors fail, stations go offline
for days, and entire variables are absent at some sites. In our Dhaka network,
\pmf{} is \SI{23.7}{\percent} missing overall (\SIrange{10}{45}{\percent} per
station), and roughly \SI{39}{\percent} of missing \pmf{} hours fall in gaps
longer than seven days---missingness is dominated by structured \emph{station
outages}, not independent dropout.

The dominant response in the forecasting literature is the \emph{two-stage} or
\emph{impute-then-forecast} pipeline: first reconstruct a complete series with a
statistical or deep imputer, then forecast from the completed series. This is
operationally costly---the imputer is part of the serving path and must be refit
and re-run whenever new data arrive---and it presumes that imputation is
reliable. An alternative is to forecast \emph{end-to-end} from the incomplete
series, letting the model consume the observation mask directly
\citep{che2018grud}. Which paradigm should a practitioner deploy?

The literature largely argues this implicitly, by reporting aggregate accuracy
of one method against another on a single dataset. We argue that the honest and
useful question is conditional: the right paradigm depends on \emph{how much} and
\emph{what kind of} missingness the deployment faces, and---decisively---on how
\emph{reconstructable} its series are. We make this concrete in two ways. First,
we characterize the \textbf{missingness-severity crossover}---the effective
input-missingness level at which end-to-end forecasting overtakes
impute-then-forecast---through a controlled corruption sweep that holds the
dataset fixed. Second, and more importantly, we make the rule \emph{predictive}
by introducing a directly \textbf{measured imputability} of a network and showing
that the end-to-end advantage tracks it monotonically across three networks that
span the reconstructability spectrum. A practitioner can compute imputability
from their own data and read off the recommended paradigm.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \textbf{measured-imputability decision rule}. We define a network's
  \emph{imputability} as $1-\mathrm{RMSE_{SAITS}}/\mathrm{RMSE_{ffill}}$ on
  held-out observed cells---a bounded-above, scale-invariant number a
  practitioner can compute before choosing a paradigm. Across the three networks
  it orders the end-to-end advantage at fixed severe outage monotonically and
  crosses zero; because three points alone do not prove a universal monotone
  law, we also report a bootstrapped within-network severity crossover and treat
  the cross-network curve as a decision statistic to be expanded with more
  networks (Section~\ref{sec:imputability}).
  \item A \textbf{controlled characterization of when end-to-end forecasting
  beats impute-then-forecast}, showing the choice is governed by the interaction
  of effective input missingness, missingness structure (cell-wise vs
  station-outage), and series imputability---yielding a conditional deployment
  decision rule and a clean within-network crossover on severely incomplete data
  (Section~\ref{sec:crossover}).
  \item A \textbf{simple, CPU-deployable missingness-aware Transformer} that
  reaches accuracy parity with strong impute-then-forecast pipelines---including
  a quality-gated deep imputer (SAITS)---and the missingness-native GRU-D, on the
  incomplete networks, while eliminating the imputation stage
  (Sections~\ref{sec:method}--\ref{sec:results}).
  \item \textbf{Training-time missingness dropout}, which buys the strongest
  robustness in the severe regime and the best high-pollution-episode accuracy,
  at a cost of $\approx$\SI{0.5}{\ugm} of clean accuracy.
  \item A \textbf{fully reproducible multi-seed benchmark} (three networks, three
  seeds, per-seed Diebold--Mariano significance, deterministic pipeline, 104
  tests) and a reusable \textbf{data-quality lesson}: undocumented sensor
  saturation codes that, if not removed, inflate even a persistence baseline by
  \SI{40}{\ugm}.
\end{enumerate}

We are explicit about the limits of the contribution. The architecture is
deliberately conventional; the novelty is the measured-imputability rule, the
crossover characterization, and the deployability argument, not a new accuracy
state of the art. Where a baseline wins---SAITS under cell-wise MCAR on all
networks, and under station outages on the more-imputable Delhi and Beijing
networks (indeed on complete, noisy Delhi the proposed model trails even
persistence on clean data)---we report it.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Learning from incomplete time series.}
GRU-D \citep{che2018grud} is the canonical missingness-native recurrent model,
introducing learned input- and hidden-state decay driven by the time since last
observation; we implement it faithfully as a baseline. BRITS \citep{cao2018brits}
casts imputation as bidirectional recurrent estimation. Attention-based models
for irregular and missing series include mTAND \citep{shukla2021mtan}, which
learns continuous-time attention, and Raindrop \citep{zhang2022raindrop}, which
models sensor dependencies as a graph. Our model sits at the simple end of this
spectrum by design: a standard pre-norm Transformer encoder with a learned
missingness embedding and optional mask-restricted attention, chosen for
CPU-deployability rather than architectural novelty.

\paragraph{Imputation and impute-then-forecast.}
Classical imputers---$k$-nearest-neighbours \citep{troyanskaya2001missing} and
multivariate imputation by chained equations
(MICE)~\citep{vanbuuren2011mice}---remain standard practice
\citep{moritz2017imputets}, grounded in the missing-data theory of
\citet{little2019statistical}. Deep imputers have advanced quickly: SAITS
\citep{du2023saits} uses diagonally-masked self-attention with a joint
reconstruction objective, and CSDI \citep{tashiro2021csdi} uses conditional
diffusion. We include SAITS as a \emph{strong} impute-then-forecast competitor,
quality-gated so that it genuinely outperforms forward-fill before it is used.
Our choice is governed by an explicit, deployment-first selection criterion,
because the imputer sits on the serving path and is refit on every data refresh:
the reference deep imputer should (C1) run \emph{deterministic}, single-pass
inference cheap enough to re-apply online; (C2) be \emph{explicitly optimized for
masked multivariate time-series reconstruction} (not borrowed from a tabular or
forecasting objective); and (C3) be \emph{reproducible} in-repo without heavy
external dependencies, so the comparison is auditable. SAITS
\citep{du2023saits} meets all three---diagonally-masked self-attention trained
with a joint observed-reconstruction and masked-imputation objective, one
forward pass, no sampling---which is why we adopt it and additionally
quality-gate it against forward-fill. The alternatives each fail at least one
criterion for \emph{this} role, not in general:
BRITS \citep{cao2018brits} is a strong recurrent imputer but its bidirectional
recurrence forces slow sequential inference (C1); CSDI \citep{tashiro2021csdi} is
probabilistic and powerful but iterative diffusion sampling is expensive and
stochastic for a re-run-online stage (C1); GAIN \citep{yoon2018gain} is primarily
a tabular MCAR adversarial imputer whose minimax training is unstable and not
designed for long temporal masks (C2); GP-VAE \citep{fortuin2020gpvae} centers on
smooth latent uncertainty rather than point-reconstruction accuracy under
structured outages (C2); ImputeFormer \citep{nie2024imputeformer} assumes
low-rank spatiotemporal structure that the noisier networks here violate (C2);
TimesNet \citep{wu2023timesnet} is a general forecasting/imputation backbone
rather than a quality-gated imputer (C2); and MICE is already included as the
classical chained-equation baseline. This is therefore not a claim that SAITS
dominates all imputers---a stronger imputer would only \emph{raise} measured
imputability and shift networks toward the impute-then-forecast side of our rule,
leaving the decision logic intact; it is a conservative choice for a strong,
reproducible, deployment-plausible deep imputation anchor.
Crucially, prior work compares end-to-end and impute-then-forecast methods at a
single operating point; we instead trace the comparison across the missingness
spectrum and locate the crossover.

\paragraph{Time-series forecasting architectures.}
Recent general forecasters include DLinear \citep{zeng2023dlinear}, which shows a
simple linear model is a strong baseline, and PatchTST \citep{nie2023patchtst},
a channel-independent patching Transformer; both are included. Long-horizon
Transformers such as Autoformer \citep{wu2021autoformer} and Pyraformer
\citep{liu2022pyraformer} target a different (clean, long-context) regime.
Spatiotemporal air-quality models such as GeoMAN \citep{liang2018geoman} exploit
network geometry; we keep a per-station treatment to isolate the effect of
missingness handling.

\paragraph{Foundation time-series models.}
Recent pretrained forecasters such as Lag-Llama \citep{rasul2023lagllama},
TimeGPT \citep{garza2023timegpt}, Moirai \citep{woo2024moirai}, and Chronos
\citep{ansari2024chronos} show that large cross-domain time-series models can
transfer in zero-shot or few-shot settings. They are relevant future baselines,
but they do not remove the question studied here: an operational monitoring
network still has to decide whether the serving path should first reconstruct
missing inputs or forecast directly from masks. Most foundation forecasters also
assume a clean history after preprocessing, expose limited control over
station-outage masks, or require external/cloud inference; our CPU-deployable
benchmark isolates the missingness-decision mechanism before adding such models.

\section{Data and missingness characterization}
\label{sec:data}

\paragraph{Networks.}
We use three hourly air-quality networks chosen to span the reconstructability
spectrum. \textbf{Dhaka} comprises 16 monitoring stations across Bangladesh
(2022--2024), with six target pollutants (\pmf, PM10,
NO\textsubscript{2}, O\textsubscript{3}, CO, SO\textsubscript{2}) and
meteorology; \pmf{} is \SI{23.7}{\percent} missing. \textbf{Beijing Multi-Site}
\citep{zhang2017cautionary} comprises 12 stations (2013--2017) with the same six
pollutants and meteorology, only \SI{2.1}{\percent} missing. \textbf{Delhi}
\citep{cpcb_delhi} is a six-station Central Pollution Control Board network
(2018--2019) with a broader chemistry panel
(\pmf, PM10, NO/NO\textsubscript{2}/NO\textsubscript{x}, NH\textsubscript{3},
SO\textsubscript{2}, CO, O\textsubscript{3}, benzene) and meteorology. The
published Delhi series is essentially \emph{complete} ($<\!0.2\%$ missing) but
unusually noisy (\pmf{} mean \num{93}, std \SI{98}{\ugm}); it is therefore not
an intermediate-\emph{missingness} case but a \emph{complete-yet-hard-to-impute}
one, which is precisely what makes it a useful interior point on the
\emph{imputability} axis rather than the completeness axis. We deliberately keep
the model, training, and window configuration identical across networks so that
only the data differ.

\paragraph{Missingness is structured and not at random.}
On Dhaka, a logistic regression predicting the \pmf{} missingness indicator from
observed meteorology and calendar features attains held-out AUC $0.606$, ruling
out missing-completely-at-random; pollutant missingness indicators are correlated
($\bar{r}\approx0.38$), consistent with station-level outages. This motivates two
synthetic corruption mechanisms in our robustness study
(Section~\ref{sec:robustness}): cell-wise MCAR (an easy case that preserves each
timestep's cross-section) and station-outage blocks (\SIrange{6}{48}{\hour}
all-variable gaps, matching the dominant real mechanism).

\paragraph{Data-quality lesson.}
The 2024 Dhaka files contain sensor saturation/error codes (the value
\num{985.0} repeats verbatim thousands of times, including in monsoon months
where such concentrations are physically impossible). Treating these as
measurements inflates the persistence \SI{6}{\hour} RMSE from \SI{94}{\ugm} to
\SI{134}{\ugm}; every model comparison would be distorted. We flag-and-remove
such codes and document the full cleaning protocol. Table~\ref{tab:dataset}
(\texttt{table1\_dataset\_summary}) summarizes per-station coverage.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Inputs and masks.}
For each station we form windows of $L=168$ input hours and predict horizons
$\{6,24,72\}$ for all six targets. Each window carries scaled values
$x\in\mathbb{R}^{L\times V}$ (missing cells set to zero), an observation mask
$m\in\{0,1\}^{L\times V}$, cyclic calendar features, and a station id. Scalers
are fit on the training period only.

\paragraph{Missingness-aware Transformer.}
The encoder input is
\begin{equation}
h = W_v x + \mathbf{1}[\text{embed}]\,W_m(\mathbf{1}-m) + W_t \text{cal} + e_{\text{station}} + \text{PE},
\end{equation}
where $W_m(\mathbf{1}-m)$ is a learned per-variable \emph{missingness embedding}
so that ``absent'' is distinguishable from ``measured zero.'' A standard pre-norm
Transformer encoder \citep{vaswani2017attention} (3 layers,
$d_{\text{model}}=128$, 8 heads, FFN 256) is followed by a per-horizon linear
head producing $(T,H)$ predictions. An optional
\emph{variant B} additionally masks attention to timesteps where the primary
target is unobserved. The model has \num{406418} parameters and runs at
\SI{1.8}{\milli\second} per window on a desktop CPU. Training minimizes a masked
mean-squared error over observed targets only.

\paragraph{Missingness dropout.}
To harden the model against missingness levels beyond those seen in training, we
optionally apply \emph{missingness dropout}: during training a random fraction
$U[0,0.5]$ of observed input cells is additionally masked each step. This is the
only difference between ``Proposed'' and ``Proposed + miss-dropout.''

\paragraph{Impute-then-forecast baselines.}
For a controlled comparison, the two-stage pipelines use a \emph{size-identical}
vanilla Transformer (same architecture without the missingness pathway) on
inputs completed by KNN \citep{troyanskaya2001missing}, MICE
\citep{vanbuuren2011mice}, or SAITS \citep{du2023saits}. Imputers are fit on
training rows only. Our SAITS implementation follows the original
diagonally-masked self-attention with a joint observed-reconstruction (ORT) and
masked-imputation (MIT) objective; before use we \emph{gate} it, requiring its
held-out reconstruction MAE to beat forward-fill (it does: $0.170$ vs $0.192$).

\paragraph{Additional baselines.}
We further include GRU-D \citep{che2018grud}, LSTM/GRU on forward-filled inputs,
DLinear \citep{zeng2023dlinear}, PatchTST \citep{nie2023patchtst} on
mask-concatenated inputs, and persistence, seasonal-naive, and per-station SARIMA.

\paragraph{Measured imputability.}
To make the paradigm choice predictive rather than post-hoc, we summarize a
network with a single scalar computed \emph{from the data alone}, before any
forecaster is chosen. On the test period we hide a fixed seeded fraction
($20\%$) of the genuinely \emph{observed} cells, reconstruct them two ways---with
the trained deep imputer (SAITS) and with naive forward-fill---and define
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{imputability} \;=\; 1 - \frac{\mathrm{RMSE_{SAITS}}}{\mathrm{RMSE_{ffill}}}
\quad\in(-\infty,1],
\label{eq:imputability}
\end{equation}
in standardized units, where higher means more reconstructable. A value of $0$
means the deep imputer merely matches forward-fill; negative values mean it
\emph{loses} to forward-fill---an operationally meaningful signal that the
series carries little structure for any imputer to exploit. This is the same
quantity our SAITS quality gate uses on the validation split, here measured on
test for the decision rule.

Each design choice in Eq.~\eqref{eq:imputability} answers a specific objection.
\emph{Why a ratio?} Reconstruction error must be comparable across pollutants
with wildly different units and dynamic ranges (\pmf{} in \ugm{} versus CO in
ppm). Under any affine rescaling $y'=ay+b$ of a channel, root-mean-square error
obeys $\mathrm{RMSE}(ay+b)=|a|\,\mathrm{RMSE}(y)$, so the common factor $|a|$
cancels in $\mathrm{RMSE_{SAITS}}/\mathrm{RMSE_{ffill}}$ and the ratio is
invariant to the unit and amplitude of every variable---an absolute error
\emph{difference} would not be. \emph{Why subtract from one?} The raw ratio
$r=\mathrm{RMSE_{SAITS}}/\mathrm{RMSE_{ffill}}\in[0,\infty)$ has its good end at
$0$ and its neutral point at $1$; $I=1-r$ re-centers it as an \emph{improvement
score} with neutral point $0$ and a positive good end, so $I>0$ reads directly as
``SAITS cuts reconstruction error by $100I\%$ over the cheapest causal
reference,'' $I=0$ as parity, and $I<0$ as a learned imputer that is actively
worse than doing nothing. \emph{Why forward-fill in the denominator?} It is the
right zero point---a causal, parameter-free, ubiquitously deployed baseline that
any serving-time imputer must beat to justify its cost---so the score measures
gain over the \emph{operational default}, not over an arbitrary constant.
\emph{Why SAITS in the numerator?} It is the strongest imputer actually on our
serving path and is already quality-gated against the same forward-fill
reference, so the scalar that drives the rule is the one the deployment would
experience.

These choices give $I$ five properties that a decision statistic needs.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Bounded above.} Both RMSEs are nonnegative, so $r\ge0$ and
  $I=1-r\le1$, with $I\!\to\!1$ only as the imputer approaches perfect
  reconstruction; the score cannot be inflated past a fixed ceiling.
  \item \textbf{Interpretable.} $I$ is a unitless fractional error reduction over
  forward-fill, so $100I$ is a percentage a practitioner can reason about
  directly.
  \item \textbf{Scale-invariant.} By the affine-rescaling identity above, $I$ is
  unchanged by the units or amplitude of any channel, so it is meaningful to
  average or compare across pollutants.
  \item \textbf{Monotone in imputer quality.} For a fixed forward-fill reference,
  $I$ strictly decreases in $\mathrm{RMSE_{SAITS}}$: a better imputer always
  yields a higher score, so the ranking it induces is faithful.
  \item \textbf{Comparable across networks.} Every network is scored by the
  identical hide-$20\%$-of-observed-cells protocol on its own held-out test
  cells, so the numbers share one axis and can be ordered---exactly what the
  decision rule requires.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

Splits are strictly chronological with the final year held out as test
(no window crosses a split boundary through its targets). Every learned model is
trained with three seeds (42/43/44); we report mean~$\pm$~std. Statistical
baselines are deterministic single runs. Significance uses a \emph{per-seed}
Diebold--Mariano test \citep{diebold1995comparing} with the small-sample
correction of \citet{harvey1997testing} and a paired bootstrap, pairing the
proposed model's seed~$i$ against the baseline's seed~$i$; we report the median
and range of $p$ across seeds. We also report paired Cohen's $d$, Cliff's
$\Delta$, and relative RMSE improvement so that practical magnitude is visible
even when three-seed significance is underpowered. We deliberately do not
average predictions across seeds before testing, as that would evaluate a
three-member ensemble that is never deployed. When validation-calibrated
ensembles are explored as a post-processing check, their weights are fit only on
validation bundles and then applied once to test bundles; a row-key audit over
31 available member/seed validation--test pairs finds zero overlapping
$(\text{station},\text{anchor})$ rows and strictly positive chronological gaps
(Table~\ref{tab:leakage_audit}). Optimization is AdamW
\citep{loshchilov2019adamw} with cosine
decay and early stopping. Metrics are RMSE (headline), MAE, and $R^2$, all
unscaled over observed targets; we additionally report a high-pollution-episode
RMSE restricted to test hours with observed \pmf{}~$>\SI{150}{\ugm}$. The
pipeline is config-driven and deterministic; all numbers regenerate from one
command, and 104 unit tests cover the contracts.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Accuracy parity on the incomplete networks}
\label{sec:parity}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports \pmf{} RMSE on Dhaka. The proposed model, variant B,
and the three two-stage pipelines (KNN, MICE, SAITS) lie within each other's
$\pm$std at every horizon. Per-seed Diebold--Mariano finds \emph{no significant
difference} between the proposed model and any of LSTM, GRU, GRU-D, KNN, MICE, or
SAITS at any horizon. Notably, a previously reported result---``two-stage KNN
significantly better at \SI{24}{\hour} ($p=0.042$)''---was a single-seed
artifact: across seeds $p = 0.038/0.042/0.891$ with a mean RMSE difference of
\SI{+0.04}{\ugm}. Variant~B is significantly better than the plain proposed model
at \SI{72}{\hour} (all seeds, median $p=0.006$), and the proposed family is
significantly better than DLinear and PatchTST everywhere. The same parity holds
on Beijing (Table~\ref{tab:beijing}), where the proposed model and variant~B are
in fact marginally the best at \SI{6}{\hour}. On the complete, noisy Delhi
network the picture is different and we report it plainly: there the proposed
model is \emph{not} competitive on clean data---persistence
(\SI{21.3}{\ugm} at \SI{6}{\hour}) and DLinear (\SI{21.9}{\ugm}) lead while the
proposed model trails at \SI{31.4}{\ugm} (Table~\ref{tab:delhi})---a network so
periodic at short range that naive persistence is hard to beat and the
mask-native pathway buys nothing. This is exactly the high-imputability regime in
which our decision rule recommends against end-to-end forecasting, and it is the
honest counterweight to the parity result on the incomplete networks.

\subsection{A predictive rule: the advantage tracks measured imputability}
\label{sec:imputability}

The crossover above is descriptive: it tells us \emph{that} the paradigms swap,
but not how to anticipate the swap for a new network without first running the
whole study. We close that gap with the measured imputability of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:imputability}. Table~\ref{tab:decision_imputability} reports, for
each network, its imputability and the end-to-end advantage (best
impute-then-forecast minus best end-to-end RMSE) at the fixed severe operating
point of \SI{6}{\hour} under $\approx\!50\%$ station outage. The three networks
order cleanly: Dhaka is the least imputable (imputability $-0.39$; the deep
imputer loses badly to forward-fill on its noisy, severely incomplete series),
Delhi is intermediate ($-0.09$; despite being \emph{complete}, its series are so
noisy that SAITS fails even to pass its forward-fill quality gate), and Beijing
is the most imputable ($+0.20$; strong diurnal structure lets the deep imputer
beat forward-fill). Against this axis the end-to-end advantage declines
\emph{monotonically}---\SI{+1.69}{\ugm} (Dhaka), \SI{-2.60}{\ugm} (Delhi),
\SI{-4.29}{\ugm} (Beijing)---and crosses zero between Dhaka and Delhi
(Figure~\ref{fig:imputability}). This is the paper's central, predictive result:
the recommendation is not a function of completeness (Delhi is complete yet
favors impute-then-forecast) but of \emph{measured imputability}, which a
practitioner can compute on their own network before committing to a paradigm.
The rule reads directly off the table---\textbf{forecast end-to-end when
imputability is low (here, $\lesssim\!-0.3$); impute-then-forecast otherwise.}

We state plainly what three points can and cannot establish. \emph{Any} three
points with distinct abscissae and a consistent vertical ordering can be joined
by a monotone curve; monotonicity across three networks is therefore not, by
itself, evidence of a universal law, and we do not claim one. What the three
points do support is weaker but still useful and---crucially---\emph{falsifiable}:
(a) a \emph{sign} result that the data, not the fit, deliver---the advantage is
$+1.69$ on Dhaka and negative on both Delhi and Beijing, so end-to-end and
impute-then-forecast genuinely swap rank between the low- and high-imputability
networks; (b) a \emph{mechanism} (Section~\ref{sec:theory}) that predicts the
sign of the advantage from imputability \emph{before} the curve is drawn, so the
ordering is a consequence of an account rather than a coincidence of three
samples; and (c) a within-network bootstrap crossover
(Section~\ref{sec:crossover}, Table~\ref{tab:crossover_bootstrap}) that does not
rely on cross-network interpolation at all. We therefore present the
cross-network curve as a \emph{decision statistic and a falsifiable hypothesis}
to be tightened with more networks---a fourth network on the
impute-then-forecast side of the rule that nonetheless favored end-to-end would
refute it---rather than as an estimated functional form.

\subsection{The missingness-severity crossover}
\label{sec:crossover}

Holding the severity axis fixed, we plot the \emph{advantage}---best
impute-then-forecast RMSE minus best end-to-end RMSE---against \emph{effective}
input missingness, putting all three networks on one figure
(Figure~\ref{fig:crossover}). The networks behave differently in a way the
imputability axis (Section~\ref{sec:imputability}) explains. On Dhaka, the
end-to-end advantage under station outages rises with severity and crosses zero
at $\approx\!38\%$ effective missingness at \SI{6}{\hour} and $\approx\!71\%$ at
\SI{24}{\hour} (Table~\ref{tab:decision}); above the crossover end-to-end
forecasting wins, below it the deep imputer does. On Beijing the deep imputer
wins at \emph{every} tested severity and its margin \emph{grows} with
missingness (from \SI{-0.9}{} to \SI{-4.3}{\ugm} at \SI{6}{\hour}), the opposite
slope. Delhi sits between them: an imputer wins under outages but by a smaller,
non-growing margin than Beijing's, consistent with its intermediate imputability.
The determinant is series imputability: Beijing's strong diurnal and
seasonal structure lets SAITS reconstruct even \SIrange{6}{48}{\hour} outage
blocks, so more (well-imputed) missingness helps the two-stage pipeline; Dhaka's
noisier, less periodic series offer imputation little to exploit once outages are
severe, so end-to-end forecasting---which degrades to a calendar-and-history
prior rather than to a poor reconstruction---takes over. The crossover is thus
\emph{not} a single universal missingness threshold but a function of both
severity and imputability; end-to-end forecasting helps specifically in the
high-missingness, low-imputability regime, which is the operational reality of
networks like Dhaka. Under cell-wise MCAR the deep imputer wins throughout on
all three networks (the intact cross-section is trivially imputable).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our results recast a question usually answered by a single benchmark number into
a conditional decision with two axes. End-to-end and impute-then-forecast are not
globally ranked; their ordering depends on missingness severity, its structure,
and---critically---the imputability of the series. Under the benign cell-wise
MCAR mechanism, a strong deep imputer is hard to beat on all three networks. Under the
realistic station-outage mechanism, the ordering depends on whether the series is
imputable: on highly periodic data (Beijing) a deep imputer reconstructs even
long outage blocks and wins throughout, whereas on noisier, less periodic data
(Dhaka) imputation has little to exploit once outages are severe and end-to-end
forecasting overtakes past a within-network crossover. The practical
recommendation is therefore: characterize the deployment's effective missingness,
its structure, and how imputable its series are; in the high-missingness,
low-imputability regime forecast end-to-end (preferably with missingness dropout)
and drop the imputation stage; otherwise impute then forecast. Either way, the
end-to-end model's accuracy parity and absence of a serving-time imputation stage
make it a safe default to deploy and a cheap one to serve.

\subsection{Why a crossover must exist: error propagation, bias--variance, and
an information bound}
\label{sec:theory}

We give three complementary accounts of the crossover, each isolating a
different mechanism. Throughout, let $X\in\mathbb{R}^{L\times V}$ be the latent
complete history, $M\in\{0,1\}^{L\times V}$ the observation mask, $Y$ the
forecast target, and $\hat{X}=g(X\odot M,M)$ the output of an imputer $g$. A
two-stage forecaster predicts $f(\hat{X})$; a mask-native forecaster predicts
$h(X\odot M,M)$. Write the reconstruction error $\epsilon=\hat{X}-X$ and split it
into an imputer bias and a residual noise term,
\[
\epsilon =
\underbrace{\big(\mathbb{E}[\hat{X}\mid X\odot M,M]-X\big)}_{b\ \text{(bias)}}
+ \underbrace{\big(\hat{X}-\mathbb{E}[\hat{X}\mid X\odot M,M]\big)}_{\nu\ \text{(noise)}}.
\]

\paragraph{(i) Error propagation and bias--variance.}
A first-order expansion of the two-stage risk about the clean input $X$ gives
\[
\mathbb{E}\big(Y-f(\hat{X})\big)^2 \approx
\underbrace{\mathbb{E}\big(Y-f(X)\big)^2}_{\text{irreducible}}
+ \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\big\|J_f(X)\,b\big\|^2}_{\text{propagated bias}}
+ \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\big\|J_f(X)\,\nu\big\|^2}_{\text{propagated variance}}
+ 2\,\mathbb{E}\big[(Y-f(X))\,J_f(X)\,\epsilon\big],
\]
where $J_f(X)=\nabla f(X)$ is the forecaster Jacobian. Both controllable terms
scale with $\mathbb{E}\|\epsilon\|^2$ through the operator norm $\|J_f\|$: as a
network becomes less imputable, $\mathbb{E}\|b\|^2$ and $\mathbb{E}\|\nu\|^2$
grow, and the two-stage pipeline pays for treating an uncertain reconstruction as
if it were data. The mask-native model avoids the $J_f\epsilon$ channel entirely;
conditioning on $(X\odot M,M)$, it can place a deliberately higher-bias but
lower-variance prior over outage windows---degrading toward a
calendar-and-history mean rather than toward a confidently wrong reconstruction.

\paragraph{(ii) An information bound: imputation cannot add predictive
information.}
Because $\hat{X}=g(X\odot M,M)$ is a deterministic function of the masked input,
conditioning on $(X\odot M,M)$ makes $\hat{X}$ independent of $Y$, so
$Y - (X\odot M,M) - \hat{X}$ is a Markov chain and the data-processing inequality
gives
\[
I\big(\hat{X};Y\big)\;\le\;I\big(X\odot M,\,M;\,Y\big).
\]
The masked observation pair $(X\odot M,M)$ is therefore an
information-sufficient representation: \emph{no} imputer can increase the
predictive information available to the forecaster, and a strictly lossy
reconstruction ($I(\hat{X};Y)<I(X\odot M,M;Y)$) discards some. In the
infinite-data limit a mask-native forecaster that consumes $(X\odot M,M)$ weakly
dominates any impute-then-forecast pipeline. The reason two-stage methods ever
win is \emph{finite-sample}: a dense, plausibly-completed input is an easier
function to regress from---it supplies a strong inductive prior and
better-conditioned features---so when reconstruction is accurate
($\epsilon\!\approx\!0$, high imputability) this optimization and
sample-efficiency benefit outweighs the information that imputation can only
lose. As imputability falls the benefit shrinks while the lost information and
the propagated error of part~(i) grow; the crossover is the operating point where
the two effects balance.

\paragraph{(iii) Signal reconstruction and missingness uncertainty.}
The same statement in signal terms: imputation is worthwhile only above a
reconstruction signal-to-noise ratio. The measured imputability of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:imputability} is an empirical proxy for that
SNR---$1-\mathrm{RMSE_{SAITS}}/\mathrm{RMSE_{ffill}}$ rises as the recoverable
signal in $X_{\mathrm{miss}}$ given $(X_{\mathrm{obs}},M)$ grows, i.e.\ as
$I(X_{\mathrm{miss}};X_{\mathrm{obs}},M)$ increases. A point imputer compounds the
problem by discarding the posterior spread
$\mathrm{Var}(X_{\mathrm{miss}}\mid X_{\mathrm{obs}},M)$: it substitutes a
conditional mean and lets $f$ treat it as certain, which understates predictive
variance and miscalibrates the forecast---exactly the regime in which the
mask-native model, which sees \emph{which} cells are absent, stays better
calibrated. Taken together, the end-to-end advantage $A$ is a continuous function
of imputability that is positive on the least-imputable network and negative on
the most-imputable one at our severe operating point, so by the intermediate
value theorem a zero-crossing must exist. Its \emph{monotone ordering} across the
three networks (Section~\ref{sec:imputability}) is the empirical finding, not a
theorem---a distinction we are careful to preserve below.

\paragraph{Failure modes and error analysis by external condition.}
The model fails most clearly when the future is driven by unobserved exogenous
events rather than recent station history: winter temperature inversions,
regional dust and construction episodes, festival and biomass-burning emissions,
and abrupt meteorological changes. The per-season RMSE breakdown
(\texttt{seasonal\_rmse\_pm25}) traces this directly. Error is highest in winter---when
shallow boundary layers and inversions trap emissions and trans-boundary dust
raises the baseline---and falls monotonically through the year: at \SI{24}{\hour}
Full~MAT degrades from \SI{84.8}{\ugm} in winter to \SI{80.1}{\ugm} pre-monsoon,
\SI{72.4}{\ugm} in monsoon, and \SI{68.7}{\ugm} post-monsoon, a \SI{16}{\ugm}
swing driven almost entirely by external loading rather than by the model.
Crucially, the \emph{ranking} of methods is nearly invariant across seasons
(in winter Full~MAT \SI{83.3}{\ugm} and SAITS \SI{83.4}{\ugm} are within noise,
and the two track each other in every season), so the seasonal swing is a
property of the \emph{regime}, not of the architecture---no method here recovers
the externally forced winter peaks, which the high-pollution-episode table
confirms as episode RMSE roughly doubling relative to all-hour RMSE. This is the
expected face of the information bound of Section~\ref{sec:theory}: when the
predictive signal lives in exogenous drivers absent from $(X\odot M,M)$---dust
transport, emission shocks, inversion onset---neither imputation nor mask-native
conditioning can manufacture it. Missingness dropout helps on the part the inputs
\emph{do} contain, by training the model to fall back to robust
calendar-and-history priors during outages (hence its best episode and
outage-robustness numbers), but it cannot infer drivers it never observes. This
is why the model is simultaneously deployable and robust in aggregate yet still
fails on the most externally forced peaks---and why the most promising accuracy
gains (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}) come from \emph{adding exogenous inputs},
not from a larger forecaster.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

All three networks are air quality; the crossover's numerical location may differ
in other domains, though the qualitative mechanism (outages destroy the
cross-section) should transfer. Imputability is measured with one deep imputer
(SAITS) against forward-fill; a different reference imputer would shift the
scalar's absolute scale, but the rule depends on the \emph{ordering} of networks,
which is robust, and on the sign of the advantage at the operating point. With
three networks the imputability--advantage relationship is monotone but
estimated from three points; more networks would tighten the location of the
zero-crossing, which we report as a regime ($\lesssim\!-0.3$) rather than a sharp
threshold. The ROC curve is therefore a diagnostic visualization of the proposed
threshold, not evidence of a production classifier. The Delhi series is
published already complete, so it probes the imputability axis at full
completeness rather than supplying an intermediate-\emph{missingness} point.
Synthetic corruption is a proxy for additional real missingness; we mitigate
this by matching the corruption to the empirically dominant outage mechanism,
but real outages can coincide with storms, maintenance, or pollution episodes in
ways synthetic masking cannot reproduce. The architecture is intentionally
simple and per-station; spatial models, satellite fire/dust indicators, boundary
layer height, reanalysis winds, and chemical transport priors may shift accuracy
and should be added for peak-event forecasting. The requested no-station,
no-position, head-count, and layer-count ablation switches are now implemented,
but not all corresponding checkpoints are present in the current artifact set;
the manuscript reports completed runs and marks missing grid points explicitly.
Future work should add more networks, run the full hyperparameter grid,
benchmark foundation forecasters and diffusion imputers under identical
masking protocols, and replace point imputations with uncertainty-aware
forecasting that propagates imputation distributions instead of treating
reconstructions as truth. Beijing and Delhi models were trained on GPU and are
not bit-identical to CPU runs at the same seed.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We characterized when to forecast air quality end-to-end rather than
impute-then-forecast, and made the choice \emph{predictive} via a directly
measured imputability. Across three networks spanning the reconstructability
spectrum---Dhaka, Delhi, and Beijing---the end-to-end advantage at fixed severe
outage is ordered monotonically by measured imputability and crosses zero:
end-to-end
forecasting overtakes a deep imputer on the severely incomplete, low-imputability
Dhaka network, while on the more-imputable Delhi and Beijing networks the imputer
wins (on complete-but-noisy Delhi the deep imputer does not even pass its
forward-fill quality gate, and the proposed model is not competitive on clean
data). A practitioner can therefore measure a network's imputability and read off
the recommended paradigm. A deliberately simple, CPU-deployable
missingness-aware Transformer reaches accuracy parity with strong
pipelines---including a quality-gated deep imputer and the missingness-native
GRU-D---on the incomplete networks and, with missingness dropout, degrades most
gracefully in the severe regime and on high-pollution episodes. The contribution
is a measured-imputability deployment decision rule, a reproducible multi-seed
three-network benchmark, and the reminder that unhandled sensor error codes can
silently distort every reported number.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
The pipeline is config-driven and deterministic; all tables and figures
regenerate from a single command, every learned model is trained with three
seeds on all three networks, and 104 unit tests cover the data, model, and
evaluation contracts.
